Migrant construction workers are increasingly dependent on digital payments but operate in conditions where standard smartphone 
interfaces fail dust-covered hands, poor connectivity, low literacy, and extreme fatigue. Existing fintech apps rely on reading, precise touch, and stable attention, 
making it difficult for workers to verify wages, file complaints, or confidently complete transactions. This leads to real financial risk: unchallenged wage deductions, 
failed or duplicated payments, and a persistent lack of trust in digital systems.

The primary interaction model uses the phone’s hardware keys (volume and power buttons) to navigate and execute core tasks. Users trigger flows through combinations 
like long-press, double-press, or sequential presses, with feedback delivered through vibration patterns and audio cues.

Failure Mode 1: Accidental or Unintended Activation
In early testing, hardware-based interactions were sometimes triggered unintentionally (e.g., phone in pocket, or while adjusting volume), 
leading to confusion or unintended actions. This is especially risky in financial contexts where accidental initiation could erode trust.
Resolution: We introduced deliberate activation thresholds (e.g., long-press + confirmation pattern), 
along with distinct vibration cues, before any critical action is executed. No transaction can be completed without a second explicit confirmation input, 
ensuring that accidental triggers never result in financial loss. Furthermore those same changes reflect in the audio cues and screen changes in the interface,
which further provide redundancies proving safer.

Failure Mode 2: Learnability and Discoverability of Key Patterns
While hardware interaction is powerful, users initially struggled to remember multi-step button combinations without guidance, especially under fatigue. 
Unlike visual interfaces, there are fewer natural cues to “discover” available actions.
Resolution: We simplified the interaction vocabulary to a small set of consistent patterns (e.g., double-press to start, long-press to confirm, single press to cycle options), 
and reinforced them with immediate haptic and audio feedback. Over time, these patterns become muscle memory—aligning with how this user already learns (repetition over reading), 
while keeping the system usable within seconds even for first-time users.
